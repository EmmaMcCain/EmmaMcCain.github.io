crown shyness (also canopy disengagment, [1] caanopy shyness, [2] or inter-crown spacing [3]) is a phenomeon observed in some tree species, in which the crowns are of fully stocked trees do not touch each other, forming a canopy with chanel-like gaps. [4][5] The phenomeon is the most prevalent aong trees of the same species, but also occurs between trees of different speices.[6] [7] There exist hypothese as to why crown shyness is an adapative behavior, and research suggets that it might inhibit spread of leaf-eating insect larvae. [8]

    Possible physiological explanations

    The exact physiological basic of crown shyness is not certain. [6] The phenomeon has been discussed in scientfic literature since the 1920s. [9] The varitey of hypothese and experimental results might suggest that there are multiple mechanisms across different speices, an example of convergent evolution [ citation needed]

    Some hypothese contend that the interdigitation of canopy branches leads to "reciprcoal purning" of adjacent tree. Trees in windy areas suffer physical damage as they collide with each other during winds. As a result of abrasions and colllisions, there is an induced crown shyness response. Studies suggest that lateral branch growth is largely uninfluenced by the neighbours until distrubed by mechanical abrasion. [10] If the crowns are artifically prevented from colliding in the winds, they gradually fill the gaps. [11] This explains instance of crown shyness between branches of the same organism. Proponets of this idea cite that shyness is particularly seen in conditions conducive to this pruning, including windy forests, stands of flexible trees, and early succession forests where branches are flexible and limited in lateral movement. [6][12] By this explanation, variable flexibility in lateral branches has a large bearing on degree of crown shyness.
